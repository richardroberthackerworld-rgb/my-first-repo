How AI Vocal Removers Actually Work: The Science of Stem Separation

SS
Shirandasu Sandeep·June 2026·8 min read

If you've ever used a vocal remover tool, you might have wondered: how does the AI actually know which part of the audio is the voice and which part is the instrument? It sounds almost magical — and honestly, just a few years ago, it was considered nearly impossible to do well.

The Old Way: Spectral Subtraction

The original approach exploited the fact that in stereo recordings, vocals are often centered in the stereo field. By subtracting the left channel from the right, you could theoretically cancel out centered vocals. The problems were obvious: mono recordings gave no separation. Modern songs with stereo-enhanced vocals produced terrible artifacts. The results sounded hollow and filtered.

Enter Deep Learning: Source Separation

Modern AI vocal removers use a U-Net deep neural network originally developed for biomedical image segmentation, repurposed for audio. The pipeline: audio is converted to a spectrogram, the network predicts a mask representing vocal content in each frequency bin, the mask is applied to isolate vocals, then an inverse STFT reconstructs the audio signal.

The Role of Training Data

Quality comes down to training data. The best models are trained on hundreds of thousands of tracks where individual stems are available from studio recordings. Meta AI's Demucs v4 now operates in both time and frequency domains simultaneously — exceptional performance even on edge cases.

Tips for Best Results

  • Use high-quality source files — FLAC or WAV produce better results than compressed MP3s.
  • Avoid heavy reverb — Reverb bleeds between frequency bins that the model uses to distinguish vocals from instruments.
  • Post-process with EQ — A notch at obvious bleed frequencies can clean up the output significantly.
Vocal removal went from a parlor trick to professional-grade in five years. The next five years will be even more dramatic.

Noise Removal for Podcasters: Complete 2026 Guide

SS
Shirandasu Sandeep · June 2026 · 7 min read

Background noise is the number one quality complaint that new podcast listeners have about indie shows. Air conditioning, keyboard clicks, street traffic, and room reverb all add up to an unprofessional sound. The good news: AI noise removal has never been more capable.

Types of Background Noise

  • Broadband noise (hiss): Consistent across all frequencies. From electrical equipment or HVAC. AI handles this extremely well.
  • Tonal noise (hum): 50Hz or 60Hz electrical interference. A notch EQ can solve this; AI catches harmonics too.
  • Intermittent noise: Dog barking, phone buzz — AI noise removal trained specifically for this is the most reliable solution.
  • Room reverb: Current AI handles mild reverb well; heavy reverb remains challenging.

Step-by-Step Workflow

  1. Record as usual — WAV at 44.1kHz or 48kHz, 16-bit minimum
  2. Do a rough edit to remove long silences
  3. Export the rough cut as WAV
  4. Open noise.7by.in and drop in your file
  5. Download cleaned audio and continue editing in your DAW

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-denoising: Creates "underwater" artifacts. Use the minimum setting needed.
  • Denoising a lossy file: Always process the original WAV, not an MP3 export.
  • Skipping mic technique: Get within 15–20cm of your mic — less post-processing needed.

MP3 vs WAV vs FLAC: Which Audio Format Should You Use?

SS
Shirandasu Sandeep · May 2026 · 5 min read

Every time you save or export audio, you're making a format decision that affects both quality and file size. The wrong choice can mean bloated files or irreversible quality loss.

MP3: The Universal Format

MP3 achieves small file sizes through lossy compression — discarding audio data human hearing is least sensitive to. Use for streaming, podcast distribution, final consumer delivery. Best bitrate: 320kbps for highest quality, 128kbps for voice.

WAV: The Production Standard

WAV is uncompressed — stores raw audio samples with no quality reduction. File sizes are large (~10MB/minute stereo 44.1kHz 16-bit) but quality is perfect. Use for recording, editing, and mastering.

FLAC: The Archival Sweet Spot

FLAC achieves 40–60% smaller files than WAV with zero quality loss. Completely lossless. Use for archiving music collections and sharing high-quality audio.

Record and edit in WAV or FLAC. Deliver to consumers as MP3. Never re-export a lossy file to another lossy format.

Why Everyone Should Use a Temp Email for Sign-Ups

SS
Shirandasu Sandeep · May 2026 · 4 min read

Every time you sign up for a new service, you hand over your email address. Some services are responsible with it. Most aren't. Your inbox fills with newsletters, promotional spam, and worse — your email gets sold to data brokers and ends up in leak databases. A temporary email address solves all of this instantly.

What Is a Temp Email?

A temporary email (also called disposable email or throwaway email) is a randomly generated inbox that you can use to receive emails for a short period — usually minutes to hours — and then discard. It works exactly like a real email address for verification purposes, but it's completely anonymous and leaves no trace back to you.

When to Use Temp Mail

  • Signing up for a free trial you're not sure you'll keep
  • Downloading a PDF or resource that requires your email
  • Participating in a survey or giveaway
  • Testing a website or app that requires registration
  • Accessing public WiFi that needs email verification

When NOT to Use Temp Mail

  • For accounts you'll need long-term (banking, government, main social accounts)
  • For anything requiring 2FA by email as a permanent security measure

The Rise of Browser-Based AI Media Tools in 2026

SS
Shirandasu Sandeep · April 2026 · 7 min read

Two years ago, running a serious AI model in a browser was a novelty. In 2026, browser-based AI processing is fast enough for professional workflows — reshaping the creative tools industry entirely.

What Changed: WebAssembly + ONNX Runtime

  • WebAssembly: Near-native execution for C/C++ and Rust code in the browser. Modern AI inference libraries compile directly to WASM.
  • ONNX Runtime Web: Microsoft's runtime with WebAssembly and WebGPU backends that run quantized AI models at near-server performance.
  • WebGPU: Direct GPU compute access from the browser — same hardware that makes AI inference fast on desktop apps.

Privacy as a Feature

When processing happens in the browser, your files never leave your device. Privacy isn't a policy promise; it's a technical reality built into the architecture.

The Cost Equation

Traditional SaaS AI tools pay for GPU server time for every file processed. Browser-based tools shift that cost to the user's own device — making accessible pricing viable without sacrificing quality.
