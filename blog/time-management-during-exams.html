Time Management in the Exam Hall: Finish Every Paper

July 4, 2026 · 5 min read · by Shirandasu Sandeep

Thousands of marks are lost every year not to ignorance but to the clock. Beating it is a learnable skill.

Budget before you write

In the first two minutes, do arithmetic, not answers: total minutes minus 10 (for review), divided across sections by marks. A 3-hour, 80-mark paper allows about two minutes per mark. Write your section deadlines on the rough sheet — "Section B by 11:20" — so drift becomes visible early, while it is still fixable.

The three-pass method

  1. Pass 1 — harvest: answer everything you find easy, in order. This banks marks fast and builds calm.
  2. Pass 2 — work: return to the moderate questions with the bulk of your remaining time.
  3. Pass 3 — fight: the hardest questions get whatever is left. Even partial steps earn partial marks.

The classic disaster — twenty-five minutes sunk into question 2 while three easy questions go unread — is impossible under this method.

Rules for the stubborn question

  • Set a hard per-question ceiling (marks × 2 minutes). At the bell, mark it and move.
  • Never erase large sections — strike one line through; examiners mark the best attempt.
  • Attempt everything. A blank scores exactly zero; a formula and first step usually don't.

Train it before you need it

Exam-hall pacing is muscle memory, built only by writing timed papers. Generate a full paper on 7Q, set a real 3-hour timer, phone outside the room — once a week until the exam. By the real day, the clock will feel like an old opponent you have already beaten five times.
