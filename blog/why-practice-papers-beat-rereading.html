Why Practice Papers Beat Re-Reading (The Testing Effect)

July 14, 2026 · 5 min read · by Shirandasu Sandeep

Cognitive science has a clear answer on the best use of a revision hour — and it is not reading the chapter again.

The illusion of fluency

Re-reading a chapter feels productive because the material seems familiar. But familiarity is not recall. In the exam hall nobody hands you the page to recognise — you must produce the answer from a blank sheet. Recognition and recall are different muscles, and re-reading trains only the weaker one.

The testing effect

Decades of studies agree: attempting to retrieve information strengthens memory far more than re-exposure to it. One landmark result found students who tested themselves retained roughly 50% more a week later than students who re-read for the same time. Wrong answers help too — a corrected mistake is remembered better than a fact never tested.

How to use this tomorrow

  1. Finish a chapter once, normally.
  2. Close the book and take a fresh question paper on that chapter — 7Q generates one with an answer key in seconds.
  3. Mark yourself. Every red mark is a precise map of what to restudy.
  4. Restudy only the gaps, then take a second, different paper two days later.

Space it out

The second multiplier is spacing: test on day 1, day 3, day 7, day 21. Each successful recall at a longer gap roughly doubles how long the memory lasts. A little practice, spread out, beats a heroic single night every time.

The practical summary

Read once, test many times. Your marks come from retrieval, so spend your hours where the marks are.
